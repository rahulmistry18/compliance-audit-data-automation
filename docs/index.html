EU · ESMA

EMIR — European Market Infrastructure Regulation

Think of it as a radar system for derivatives. Every time a bank, fund, or company trades a derivative (a contract whose value depends on something else — an interest rate, a currency, a commodity), EMIR requires that trade to be reported to an approved trade repository, generally by the next business day.

Regulators use this to see risk building up across the whole market, instead of finding out only once something has already gone wrong.

In one line: does the regulator know this derivative trade happened?

EU · ESMA

MiFID II — Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II

The EU's much broader rulebook for how financial markets operate day to day: what must be disclosed about a trade (price, timing, venue, counterparty), best-execution duties (firms must get clients a fair deal, not just a convenient one), and market transparency.

This project checks a simplified slice of MiFID II's transaction-reporting rule (RTS 22): a defined set of fields — instrument ID, timestamp, venue, price, counterparty LEI — must be complete.

In one line: was this trade conducted properly, and is it fully documented?

Why this matters in practice: EMIR and MiFID II overlap but aren't the same thing — a trade can be perfectly EMIR-compliant (reported on time) while still failing MiFID II's field-completeness requirement, or vice versa. Treating them as one rule would hide half the findings a real audit would catch.

The pattern worth noticing

Different deadlines, converging data standards

The US wants near-real-time visibility into the world's largest derivatives market. The EU sits at T+1. Asia-Pacific regulators deliberately relaxed to T+2 to ease implementation across multiple domestic regimes.

But since 2024–2025, all of them have converged on the same underlying data fields — Unique Transaction Identifiers (UTI), Unique Product Identifiers (UPI), and Critical Data Elements (CDE) — set by the international standard-setters CPMI and IOSCO. That's what CDE-001 in this project checks, regardless of jurisdiction.

Figures simplified for teaching purposes and reflect publicly available regulatory guidance as of mid-2026 (ESMA, UK FCA/Bank of England, US CFTC/SEC, ASIC, HKMA/SFC, MAS). Not legal advice — always check current primary regulatory text before relying on any deadline or field list operationally.
