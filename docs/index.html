New to this? Two rules do most of the work here: EMIR and MiFID II. Both are European rules, but the same idea shows up under different names in the US and Asia - explained below.

Rule 1

EMIR - "did anyone tell the regulator this trade happened?"

Imagine every time a bank makes a certain kind of trade, it has to send a text message to a regulator saying "this happened." EMIR is that rule. It exists so regulators can see risk building up across the whole market, instead of finding out only after something goes wrong.

The deadline is usually the next business day. If a trade is reported late, that's a problem this tool catches.

Plain version: does the regulator know this trade happened, on time?

Rule 2

MiFID II - "was this trade done properly and written down correctly?"

This is a bigger rulebook covering how trading should work in general: fair prices, honest dealing, and keeping complete records - who traded what, when, where, and at what price.

This tool checks a simple version of that: are the key details (what was traded, when, where, at what price, and who the counterparty was) actually filled in?

Plain version: was this trade done properly, and is the paperwork complete?

How the checks map to real rules

This check……is really asking…based on
Was it reported on time?Did someone tell the regulator about this trade before the deadline?EMIR in Europe, similar rules in the US and Asia - deadlines differ by country
Is the paperwork complete?Are the trade's key details filled in - what, when, where, price, who?MiFID II in Europe, now a shared global standard everywhere
Is the counterparty properly identified?Does the other party to the trade have a valid ID code?A single global ID system used everywhere, not just Europe
Basic bookkeepingNo duplicate entries, no entries that don't add upOrdinary accounting hygiene, not a specific regulation
Contract cancellation checksWas a client contract cancelled properly, with the right notice?Internal company policy, not a specific regulation
Why bother checking both separately? A trade can be reported on time (fine by rule 1) but still be missing details (a problem under rule 2), or the other way around. Treating them as one check would hide half the real problems.

The rule "report the trade on time" exists almost everywhere - it's just called something different, and the deadline is different, depending on the country.

The interesting part

Different deadlines, same paperwork

The US wants to know almost immediately, since it's the biggest market. Europe gives a day. Asia-Pacific gives two days, on purpose, to make the rule easier to follow.

But since 2024-2025, everyone agreed on the same required details - the same fields, the same ID formats - even though the deadlines are different. That agreement is what the "is the paperwork complete" check in this tool is based on.

Short version: the trades and contracts on this dashboard are made up. The rules and deadlines they're checked against are real. Here's exactly what that means.

Trade & contract records

0% real

All 31 trades and 12 contracts are fabricated for this demo. No real client, trade, or company appears anywhere in this dataset.

Rules & deadlines applied

100% real

The reporting deadlines, required fields, and regulator names come from actual public regulatory text (EMIR, MiFID II, Dodd-Frank, ASIC, HKMA/SFC, MAS).

Why show fake data at all? This project is a proof of concept demonstrating how the checking process works, not a live audit of anyone's real trades. Using fabricated data means it can be shared publicly (in a portfolio, for example) with zero privacy or confidentiality risk, while the rules being demonstrated are the genuine ones companies have to follow.
